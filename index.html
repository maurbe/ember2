Abstract

Galaxy formation is a complex problem that connects large scale cosmsology with small scale astrophysics. Hydrodynamical simulations that follow the baryonic physics exactly are the most sophisticated approach to model galaxy formation, but require large computational costs. Recently, modern emulation techniques based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have been trained to emulate baryonic fields directly from dark matter simulations. While these emulators have been shown to capture the relevant physical information, they drastically reduced the involved computational expenses. However, due to their fixed kernel size, basic CNNs fail to capture the multi-scale nature of galaxy formation that ranges from the cosmic web on Mpc scales to galactic scales of sub-kpc. Training basic CNNs over large redshift ranges is challenging, due to the increasing non-linearity of the cosmic fields paired with the memory inefficiency of CNNs. This work introduces EMBER-2, an improved version of the EMBER (EMulating Baryonic EnRichment) framework to emulate baryonic fields over a large redshift range, from z=12 to z=1, at a pixel resolution of 450 cpc/h resolving the interstellar medium (ISM) of individual galaxies. EMBER-2 incorporates a context-based styling network paired with Modulated Convolutions for fast, accurate and memory efficient emulation. Although EMBER-2 uses almost 4 times less trainable parameters than the previous version, the model drastically improves in every tested summary metric including mass conservation (maximum errors of 1%) and cross-correlation coefficients (maximum error of 5%) over the entire redshift range. Furthermore, EMBER-2 emulates galaxies that are consistent with the simulated sample in terms of morphological indicators like the mass-size relation and the clumping factor. The EMBER-2 framework builds the foundation for emulating the entire baryonic sector of galaxies, producing mock catalogues that can directly be incorporated in future analysis pipelines.

Architecture

The following figure depicts the main architectural design choices of the neural network models. a) A single block in the decoder branch of the synthesis network comprised of a bicubic upsampling step, followed by a concatenation with the skip connection from the corresponding encoder block. The base kernels of the two ModConvs are modulated via an affine layer with the externally mapped style vector w. Between the two convolutions we concatenate 8 channels of gaussian noise to the tensor before feeding it through the second ModConv. Note that the two affine mappings are learned individually. b) Total overview of the synthesis network based on the typical U-Net architecture. Features from the input x are extracted via an encoder (blue blocks) comprised of two normal convolutions and a strided convolution for pooling. Subsequently the representations are decoded through a series of ModConv blocks to synthesize y. The Mapping network shown in purple is a simple 8-layer MLP (each layer of size 64) that maps the context tuple (z,r) to the style vector w. c) Discriminator architecture comprised of individual blocks featuring a two-path strategy of convolutions in real and fourier space. The last convolution outputs a single scalar representing the score of the discriminator. d) Discriminator block comprised of two normal convolutions on the right path. Along the left path the data is first transformed to fourier space, convolved to extract frequency features and subsequently transformed back to real space via the inverse fourier transform. The data from the left and right path are added elemet-wise and pooled by a strided convolution. Note that throughout all networks, we use kernel sizes of 3, strides of 1 (except for the strided convolution, where a stride of 2 is used) and LeakyReLU activation functions.
